Overview

Law IX identified lambda(N) = d_min_init / merge_thr as the control
parameter governing the temporal regime and geometric complexity of the
unified manifold.  But the Law IX consolidation sweep revealed that
lambda alone achieves only 61.3% mechanism accuracy.  The mechanism
layer -- which specific collapse pathway a given parameter cell
follows -- is not determined by lambda.  It is determined by the
launch angle gamma.

Law X formalizes this complementary structure.  Where Law IX explains
when collapse happens (instantly if lambda << 1, slowly if lambda >> 1),
Law X explains how collapse happens (inward if gamma > 0, outward if
gamma < 0, stalled if gamma ~ 0).  Together, lambda and angle form the
two-coordinate generative system of the ED-Arch unified manifold: a
temporal coordinate that sets the complexity regime and an angular
coordinate that selects the mechanism identity within that regime.


Formal Statement

Law X -- The Angle Partition and Boundary-Layer Geometry:

The launch angle gamma is the geometric selector of mechanism identity
across all N.  Positive angles produce inward-collapse mechanisms,
negative angles produce outward-PBC mechanisms, and the narrow
boundary layer near gamma ~ -0.5 degrees hosts the IC/non-IC phase
transition.  This angle partition is topologically protected: it
persists unchanged from N = 4 to N = 128, survives the lambda = 1
crossover, and cannot be erased by any increase in particle count.
Lambda determines the temporal regime; angle determines the mechanism
partition; together they generate the full structure of the unified
manifold.


Conceptual Framing

Laws I through IX describe the manifold's dependence on particle
count N, which controls complexity through the lambda invariant.  But
the manifold has a second axis that N does not touch.  At every value
of N -- from N = 4 where the manifold is folded and cusp-rich, to
N = 128 where it is a featureless hyperplane -- the parameter space
is partitioned into the same three angular regions: an inward-collapse
domain at positive angles, an outward-PBC domain at negative angles,
and a thin boundary layer near the tangent direction where mechanism
identity is decided by sub-degree angular perturbations.

This partition is not a dynamical consequence of the simulation.  It
is a geometric consequence of the velocity decomposition.  Each
particle's initial velocity is the sum of a tangential component
(proportional to cos(gamma)) and a radial component (proportional to
sin(gamma) plus drift).  When gamma > 0, the radial component points
inward, the ring contracts, and particles merge by approaching the
center: inward-collapse.  When gamma < 0, the radial component points
outward, the ring expands, and particles merge by encountering PBC
walls: outward-PBC.  When gamma is near zero, the radial component is
negligible, and mechanism identity depends on whichever secondary
effect -- drift, PBC geometry, ring-to-box ratio -- breaks the
near-tangential symmetry first.

Lambda controls how long the trajectory lasts before merger.  Angle
controls which direction the trajectory points.  These are
independent coordinates on the manifold, and neither can substitute
for the other.  Law IX's 61.3% mechanism accuracy reflects this
irreducibility: lambda correctly predicts the temporal regime (99.9%
at lambda < 1) but cannot predict which of the angularly-protected
mechanism sectors a given cell occupies.


Geometric Interpretation

The angle partition divides the (angle, radius, drift) parameter
space into three sectors whose boundaries are set by the velocity
decomposition, not by N or lambda.

The inward sector (gamma > 0) is the IC domain.  Every particle has
a net inward radial velocity component, and the ring contracts toward
its center.  In the lambda >> 1 regime (N = 4), the contraction is
slow and the trajectory may cross PBC walls, bounce off corners, or
wind through the periodic lattice before merger -- producing the rich
sub-mechanism taxonomy of IC-bounced, IC-delayed, and CR-diagonal.
In the lambda << 1 regime (N >= 48), contraction is instantaneous and
the only surviving IC sub-type is IC-direct (48.8% of all cells), with
IC-bounced persisting at 6.4% as a radius-dependent geometric
correction when the circumradius places particles near PBC walls.
The inward sector accounts for 92.3% of all positive-angle cells at
N >= 20 and approaches 100% at N >= 32 for angles above +0.5 degrees.

The outward sector (gamma < 0) is the OPBC domain.  Every particle
has a net outward radial velocity component, and the ring expands
until particles encounter PBC walls and merge through periodic-image
interactions.  In the lambda >> 1 regime, the expansion trajectory is
complex: particles may wind through multiple PBC cells, glance off
edges, or spiral through corner sequences -- producing OPBC-winding,
OPBC-glancing, and CR-multi sub-types.  In the lambda << 1 regime,
expansion is immediate (particles are already near or past PBC walls
due to the large ring) and the dominant sub-type is OPBC-single
(28.3% of all N >= 48 cells).  The outward sector accounts for 100%
of cells at gamma <= -3 degrees across all N >= 20.

The boundary layer (gamma ~ -0.5 to +0.5 degrees) is the
mechanism-selection zone.  Here the radial velocity component is of
order sin(0.5 degrees) ~ 0.009 in dimensionless units -- comparable
to the drift perturbation scale (d = 0.003 to 0.030) and to the
PBC-induced velocity corrections from the ring-to-box geometry.  In
this narrow angular strip, mechanism identity is not determined by the
dominant term in the velocity decomposition (which is tangential) but
by the competition among sub-leading terms: the small inward or
outward radial component, the drift perturbation, and the geometric
asymmetry of the PBC box.  The boundary layer is where DECAY lives
at N = 4, where OL-stalled and OL-wandering orbits concentrate, and
where drift perturbations can flip mechanism labels even when collapse
is instantaneous.


The True IC Boundary

The IC/non-IC phase boundary does not lie at gamma = 0.  It lies near
gamma ~ -0.5 degrees, and its precise location shifts with radius and
PBC geometry.

This offset is a consequence of the velocity normalization.  The raw
velocity of each particle is

    v_raw = cos(gamma) * tangent + (sin(gamma) + drift) * (-radial)

which is then normalized to unit magnitude.  At gamma = 0 with zero
drift, the velocity is purely tangential and no radial contraction
occurs: this produces OL-stalled, not inward-collapse.  But the
ring's initial geometry introduces a small asymmetry.  Particles on
a finite ring in a PBC box are not in a perfectly symmetric
tangential configuration: the curvature of the ring, the
discretization of particle positions, and the proximity of PBC walls
all introduce perturbations that can break the tangential degeneracy
in the inward direction.  The result is that IC mechanisms persist
at small negative angles (gamma = -0.5 degrees) where the nominal
radial component is outward but the geometric perturbations are
inward.

At gamma = -0.5 degrees across N = 48 to 128, the IC fraction ranges
from 33% to 68% -- a mixed zone where some radius-drift combinations
produce IC and others produce OPBC or OL.  By gamma = -3 degrees,
the IC fraction drops to zero at all N >= 20.  By gamma = +0.5
degrees, the IC fraction reaches 91-100% at N >= 32.  The boundary
is a narrow strip approximately 1 degree wide, centered near -0.25
degrees, that separates two topologically distinct mechanism sectors.

The boundary's location does not converge to a fixed angle as N
increases.  At N = 20, the mixed zone at -0.5 degrees shows 47% IC.
At N = 128, it shows 36% IC.  The boundary fluctuates with N
because the PBC geometry of the ring changes: at different N values,
different particles land near different PBC features (edges, corners,
cell boundaries), and these features pull the effective boundary
slightly inward or outward.  This fluctuation is bounded -- the
boundary never moves outside the [-1, +0.5] degree range at any
tested N -- but it does not converge to a single point.


Temporal Clause

Lambda and angle interact in the temporal layer but do not confound.

In the lambda >> 1 regime (N = 4), collapse time chi varies strongly
with both angle and radius.  The angular dependence is structured:
positive angles produce moderate chi (1-10), negative angles produce
faster chi (0.5-3) because outward expansion encounters PBC walls
sooner than inward contraction reaches the center, and the tangent
region (gamma = 0) produces the slowest dynamics (chi = 100 for DECAY,
chi > 10 for OL-wandering).  The temporal manifold at N = 4 carries
a 41 chi-unit cliff at the tangent discontinuity, 239 metastable
cells, and structured gradients that reflect the angle partition's
interaction with the folded boundary.

In the lambda << 1 regime (N >= 48), the temporal layer is trivial.
Chi is at the measurement floor (0.01) for 100% of cells regardless
of angle.  The angle partition persists -- positive angles still
produce IC, negative angles still produce OPBC -- but it carries no
temporal signature.  Both IC-direct and OPBC-single collapse within
a single timestep.  The temporal distinction between mechanism sectors
is erased by instantaneous collapse even though the mechanism
distinction itself survives.

The temporal scaling exponent chi ~ N^(-2.81) measured on the
extended dataset (34,797 cells including non-canonical parameters) is
steeper than the canonical -2.33 measured on the original grid.  This
steepening reflects the inclusion of non-canonical radii (r = 0.025
to 0.825) that sample a broader range of lambda values and weight the
fit toward faster-collapsing configurations.  The exponent is a
property of the radius distribution of the measurement grid, not a
universal constant.


Drift Clause

Drift rigidity saturates at approximately 83% at large N, and the
angle partition explains why it does not reach 100%.

In the bulk of the inward sector (gamma >= +1 degree), the radial
velocity component sin(gamma) ~ 0.017 or larger dominates the drift
perturbation (d <= 0.030), and mechanism identity is rigid across all
tested drifts.  In the bulk of the outward sector (gamma <= -1
degree), the same argument applies with reversed sign.  These two
sectors account for approximately 82% of all (angle, radius) groups
at large N, and they are drift-rigid by construction: the angle-based
velocity decomposition overwhelms the drift correction.

The remaining 18% of groups lie in the boundary layer (gamma between
-1 and +0.5 degrees, plus gamma = 0).  Here the radial velocity
component is comparable to or smaller than the drift perturbation.
A drift of d = 0.01 adds a radial component of 0.01 to the
velocity, which at gamma = 0 is the only radial component present.
This tips the balance from OL-stalled to inward-collapse.  At
gamma = -0.5 degrees, the nominal outward radial component is
sin(0.5 degrees) ~ 0.009, and a drift of d = 0.01 overwhelms it,
converting the cell from OPBC to IC.  The boundary layer is where
drift has leverage, and the 83% rigidity saturation is the
complement of the boundary layer's angular width relative to the
full angle grid.

Drift rigidity increases monotonically with lambda, not because
lambda affects the angle partition, but because lambda affects
whether the trajectory is long enough for drift to accumulate.  At
lambda >> 1 (N = 4), drift perturbations accumulate over hundreds
of timesteps and can redirect trajectories far from their initial
sector, producing only 25.5% rigidity.  At lambda << 1 (N >= 48),
collapse is instantaneous, drift has no time to accumulate beyond a
single timestep, and the only cells affected are those in the
boundary layer where even a single-timestep drift perturbation
changes the sign of the effective radial velocity.  Lambda sets the
drift-rigidity floor; the angle partition determines where the
remaining drift sensitivity lives.


Boundary-Layer Clause

The boundary layer near gamma = 0 is the structural origin of every
exception, anomaly, and residual complexity in the asymptotic regime.

DECAY occupies the exact center of the boundary layer: gamma = 0,
drift = 0, N = 4.  It requires both zero radial velocity (tangential
launch) and long trajectories (lambda >> 1).  When lambda drops below
1, the trajectory is too short for DECAY; when angle deviates from
zero, the radial component is too large for tangential stalling.
DECAY is the lambda >> 1 limit of the boundary-layer physics: the
only mechanism that requires both coordinates to take their extreme
values simultaneously.

OL-stalled is the lambda << 1 descendant of the boundary layer.  At
gamma = 0 with instantaneous collapse, particles are launched
tangentially and merge immediately through PBC proximity, producing
a mechanism that is neither inward nor outward but geometrically
stalled.  OL-stalled accounts for 7.1% of all N >= 48 cells, exactly
the fraction of the angle grid that samples gamma = 0.

The N = 8 OL-wandering resonance (22.6%) is a boundary-layer
phenomenon amplified by the lambda ~ 1 critical condition.  At
lambda ~ 1, trajectories are long enough for marginally-stable
orbits to develop but short enough that only the near-tangential
orbits (those in the boundary layer) survive to wander.  The
resonance is confined to the boundary layer's angular width,
amplified by the lambda ~ 1 timescale, and extinguished by
lambda < 1 (at N >= 12, OL-wandering drops to 1.4%).

The 17-18% drift-fragile residual at large N is a boundary-layer
effect.  It is concentrated at gamma = 0 and gamma = -0.5 degrees,
where the radial velocity component is small enough for drift to
overwhelm.  This residual does not decrease with increasing N
because it is not a lambda-dependent quantity: it is a property of
the angle grid's sampling of the boundary layer, which is independent
of N.


Taxonomy Clause

The sub-mechanism taxonomy at large N freezes to a 5-to-6-member band
whose composition is explained entirely by the angle partition and its
geometric corrections.

Three sub-types are angle-protected cores -- they exist because each
angular sector has a minimal-complexity collapse pathway that cannot
be eliminated:

    IC-direct (48.8% at N >= 48): the minimal IC pathway.  Particles
    contract inward and merge without crossing any PBC wall.  This is
    the dominant mechanism at all positive angles where the circumradius
    is small enough that the ring fits within the PBC box.

    OPBC-single (28.3% at N >= 48): the minimal OPBC pathway.
    Particles expand outward and merge through a single PBC wall
    crossing.  This is the dominant mechanism at all negative angles.

    OL-stalled (7.1% at N >= 48): the minimal boundary-layer pathway.
    Particles launch tangentially and merge through PBC proximity
    without developing a clear inward or outward trajectory.  This is
    the only mechanism at gamma = 0.

Three additional sub-types appear as radius-dependent geometric
corrections that modulate the core pathways without creating new
angular sectors:

    IC-bounced (6.4% at N >= 48): IC-direct with one or more PBC wall
    crossings during the inward contraction.  Appears when the
    circumradius is large enough that the contracting ring grazes a
    PBC wall before merger.

    OPBC-glancing (3.2% at N >= 48): OPBC-single with a small number
    of PBC crossings that deflect but do not fundamentally redirect
    the outward trajectory.  Appears at intermediate radii.

    CR-diagonal (6.2% at N >= 48): a PBC-corner mechanism that
    appears when the ring-to-box geometry places particles near the
    diagonal corners of the PBC cell.  This is a geometric correction
    whose frequency oscillates with N as the circumradius sweeps
    through resonant box-ratios.

No new sub-types appear at any N from 4 to 128.  The only sub-types
that are lost are DE-tangent and DE-frozen (DECAY variants confined
to N = 4 by Law VII).  The taxonomy does not converge to a single
sub-type because the angle partition prevents it: IC-direct cannot
absorb OPBC-single (they occupy different angular sectors) and
neither can absorb OL-stalled (which occupies the boundary layer).
The 5-to-6-member band is the irreducible minimum set by the
velocity decomposition's three-sector geometry.


Exceptions and Degeneracies

The IC fraction at positive angles does not reach 100% at any N.  At
gamma = +0.5 degrees, N = 64 shows 91% IC rather than 100%.  The
missing 9% reflects radius-specific cases where the circumradius
places particles near PBC walls and the small inward radial component
at +0.5 degrees is insufficient to complete contraction before a PBC
interaction redirects the collapse.  This is a finite-size effect
that oscillates with N and does not represent a systematic trend.

The boundary at gamma ~ -0.5 degrees is not a sharp line but a mixed
zone approximately 1 degree wide.  Within this zone, the IC fraction
varies from 33% to 68% depending on N, radius, and drift.  The
boundary cannot be expressed as a single-valued function of any
parameter because it depends on the full PBC geometry of the ring at
each (N, radius) combination.  This irreducible complexity of the
boundary layer is the structural reason that the overall mechanism
prediction accuracy saturates at 61.3% when only lambda and angle-sign
are used as predictors: the boundary layer resists binary
classification.

At N = 4 (lambda >> 1), the angle partition is obscured by trajectory
complexity.  The folded boundary produces IC cells at negative angles
and non-IC cells at positive angles for specific radii, violating the
simple angle-sign rule.  Law X's angle partition is a clean predictor
only in the lambda <= 1 regime (N >= 12); at lambda >> 1, it is
overridden by the trajectory-dependent boundary folding described in
Law VIII.  The angle partition is the asymptotic law; the N = 4
departures are the lambda >> 1 corrections.


Closing Synthesis

Law X completes the two-coordinate description of the ED-Arch unified
manifold.

Law IX showed that lambda governs the temporal regime: when collapse
happens, how complex the manifold is, and whether drift has time to
act.  Law X shows that angle governs the mechanism partition: which
collapse pathway each cell follows, which sub-mechanism it belongs to,
and where the phase boundary lies.  These are the two irreducible
coordinates of the manifold.  Lambda is the complexity dial; angle is
the mechanism selector.  Together they generate the full structure
catalogued in Laws I through VIII.

The angle partition is topologically protected.  Increasing N reduces
lambda, which erases temporal structure, collapses taxonomy, and
rigidifies drift sensitivity -- but it cannot erase the angle
partition because the partition is set by the velocity decomposition,
not by the dynamics.  As long as particles are launched with a
direction, that direction determines the mechanism sector.  The inward
sector, the outward sector, and the boundary layer between them are
geometric features of the initial conditions, not dynamical features
of the evolution.  They persist from N = 4 to N = 128 and will
persist to N = infinity.

The boundary layer near gamma ~ -0.5 degrees is the last refuge of
complexity in the asymptotic regime.  When lambda << 1 and every
temporal, geometric, and taxonomic observable has converged to its
trivial limit, the boundary layer still carries structure: a mixed
IC/OPBC zone, a drift-sensitive strip, and the irreducible ambiguity
of near-tangential launch.  The 83% drift-rigidity ceiling, the 6
surviving sub-types, the non-convergence of the boundary angle -- all
are boundary-layer phenomena, and all are consequences of the velocity
decomposition's three-sector geometry interacting with the PBC box.

The ED-Arch phase manifold is generated by two coordinates: lambda
tells it when to collapse, and angle tells it how.
