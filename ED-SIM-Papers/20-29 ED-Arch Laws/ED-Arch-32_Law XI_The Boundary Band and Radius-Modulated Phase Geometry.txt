Overview

Law X established that the launch angle gamma is the geometric selector
of mechanism identity: positive angles produce inward-collapse, negative
angles produce outward-PBC, and the narrow boundary layer near
gamma ~ -0.5 degrees hosts the phase transition between them.  But the
Law X consolidation sweep revealed that this boundary is not a line.
It is a geometric band -- a finite-width transition zone whose center,
width, and internal structure depend on the ring-to-box radius, the PBC
geometry, and the drift perturbation.  The band persists across all
tested N values from 4 to 128, does not converge to a fixed angle as
N increases, and carries the last surviving geometric structure in the
lambda << 1 asymptotic hyperplane.

Law XI formalizes the boundary band as the third structural element of
the ED-Arch generative system.  Laws IX and X describe the manifold as
generated by two coordinates: lambda for temporal regime, angle for
mechanism identity.  Law XI describes what happens at the seam between
the two mechanism sectors, where neither coordinate alone determines the
outcome and the full PBC geometry of the ring intrudes as a third --
weak but irreducible -- modulator.


Formal Statement

Law XI -- The Boundary Band and Radius-Modulated Phase Geometry:

The IC/non-IC phase boundary is a geometric band, not a line.  Its
center lies between approximately -0.75 and +0.25 degrees, its width
expands or contracts with radius and PBC proximity, and drift-induced
mechanism flips within the band are 4.9 times more frequent than in the
bulk.  The band persists across all N from 4 to 128, does not converge
to a single angle as N increases, and constitutes the last surviving
geometric feature of the lambda << 1 asymptotic hyperplane.  Radius
acts as a weak third coordinate in the generative system, modulating
the boundary band's position by up to +/-0.25 degrees across the
tested radius range.


Conceptual Framing

Laws IX and X partition the manifold into a temporal coordinate
(lambda) and a mechanism coordinate (angle).  This partition works
cleanly in the bulk: at angles above +1 degree, IC dominates at 94.5%
for N >= 20; at angles below -1 degree, non-IC dominates at 96.7%.
But between these two sectors lies a strip of parameter space
approximately 1 to 2 degrees wide where mechanism identity is not
determined by the sign of the radial velocity component alone.  In this
strip, the radial velocity v_r = sin(gamma) + drift is small enough --
of order 0.01 or less in dimensionless units -- that the PBC geometry
of the ring, the drift perturbation, and the radius-dependent proximity
to box edges and corners all contribute comparably to the collapse
dynamics.  The mechanism outcome at any point in this strip is the
result of a competition among these sub-leading effects, not the
dominance of a single geometric factor.

Law X identified this strip as the "boundary layer" and reported its
properties descriptively: mixed IC fractions, drift sensitivity,
non-convergence with N.  Law XI promotes the boundary layer from a
descriptive observation to a structural law by identifying its three
defining components -- center, width, and drift sensitivity -- and
showing that each depends on the radius in a systematic way.  The
boundary layer is not an artifact of finite angular sampling or
insufficient N.  It is a geometric feature of the velocity
decomposition interacting with the PBC box, and it cannot be eliminated
by any increase in particle count.

The conceptual shift from Law X to Law XI is analogous to the shift
from Law VIII to Law IX.  Law VIII described the complexity-reduction
flow as a phenomenological trend; Law IX identified lambda as its
structural origin.  Law X described the boundary layer as an exception
to the angle partition; Law XI identifies it as a geometric band with
systematic radius dependence and establishes that it is the irreducible
residual of the two-coordinate system.


Geometric Interpretation

The boundary band occupies the angular region where the radial velocity
component v_r = sin(gamma) + drift is comparable to the geometric
perturbations introduced by the PBC box.

At gamma = -0.5 degrees with zero drift, v_r = sin(-0.5) ~ -0.0087.
This is the nominal outward radial component, but it is small compared
to the velocity perturbations induced by PBC proximity.  When the ring
circumradius places particles near a box edge, the nearest PBC image
lies in the inward direction, and the effective radial component is
biased inward by the image interaction.  When particles are far from
edges, the nominal v_r dominates and the mechanism is OPBC.  The result
is a mixed zone: some radius-drift combinations produce IC and others
produce OPBC at the same angle and N.

The boundary band has three structural components:

The center is the angle at which the IC fraction crosses 50%.  Across
N = 48 to 128, the center lies between approximately -0.25 and 0
degrees at zero drift.  This is offset from gamma = 0 because the
ring's curvature introduces a small systematic inward bias: particles
on a convex ring are slightly closer to each other along the inward
direction than along the outward direction, and this geometric
asymmetry shifts the effective zero-radial-velocity angle slightly
into the nominal outward sector.  The center does not converge to a
fixed angle because the ring's PBC geometry changes with N: at each N,
different particles land at different positions relative to PBC
features, and the integrated inward bias fluctuates.

The width is the angular range over which the IC fraction transitions
from near-zero to near-unity.  At N >= 48, the transition spans from
approximately -0.75 degrees (26-35% IC) to +0.5 degrees (90-100% IC),
giving a width of roughly 1.25 degrees.  At smaller N, the width is
broader because trajectory complexity in the lambda > 1 regime smears
the boundary: at N = 12, the transition spans from -1 degree (20% IC)
to +1 degree (59% IC), giving a width of roughly 2 degrees.  The width
narrows with increasing N but does not collapse to zero because the
PBC-induced perturbations that drive the boundary's internal structure
are independent of lambda.

The drift sensitivity is the probability that a drift perturbation
changes the mechanism identity at a given angle.  Within the boundary
band, drift-induced flips occur at 56% of all tested (angle, radius,
N) groups, compared to 11.5% in the bulk -- a ratio of 4.9.  Peak flip
rates of 84.8% occur at angles between -0.75 and 0 degrees, where the
nominal v_r is smallest and any drift perturbation can overwhelm it.
Outside the band, drift flips are rare (1-3% at angles beyond
+/-3 degrees) and occur only at pathological radius-PBC coincidences.


Radius-Modulation Clause

Radius is the primary modulator of the boundary band's internal
structure.  The IC/non-IC boundary shifts by up to +/-0.25 degrees
across the 17 tested radii (r = 0.025 to 0.950) at fixed N and drift.

At N = 64 with zero drift, the radius cross-section reveals the
modulation pattern.  At most radii, the IC boundary lies between +0.25
and +0.75 degrees: r = 0.050 and r = 0.150 produce IC at -0.25
degrees, while r = 0.100, r = 0.200, r = 0.275, r = 0.300, and
r = 0.400 require +0.75 degrees before IC appears.  The outlier is
r = 0.500, where no IC appears at any boundary-layer angle from -1 to
+1 degree -- the boundary is pushed entirely above +1 degree.  This
is because r = 0.500 places the ring circumradius at exactly half the
box size, producing a maximally symmetric PBC configuration where all
particles are equidistant from the nearest box edge and the inward
geometric bias vanishes.

The radius modulation has two structural origins:

Edge proximity.  When the circumradius places particles near a box
edge, the nearest PBC image lies inward, biasing the effective v_r
toward contraction.  This pulls the IC boundary to more negative
angles -- the IC sector expands.  At r = 0.150 (N = 64), where the
ring is small and particles are distant from all edges, this effect is
weak and IC requires more positive angles.  At r = 0.600, where the
ring is large and particles straddle PBC walls, the effect is strong
and IC appears at smaller angles.

Corner proximity.  When the circumradius places particles near a box
corner, the two nearest PBC images lie along the diagonal, creating a
competing geometric effect that can redirect collapse into
corner-mediated (CR) pathways.  The CR-diagonal sub-type accounts for
4.7% of all N >= 48 cells and is concentrated at radii where the
circumradius-to-box ratio places particles near the 45-degree diagonal.
Corner proximity widens the boundary band by introducing a third
competing mechanism that is neither purely inward nor purely outward.

Radius is a weak third coordinate.  It does not create new mechanism
sectors or alter the fundamental angle partition; it modulates the
boundary between the two existing sectors.  The modulation amplitude
(+/-0.25 degrees) is small compared to the angular width of each
sector (roughly 10 degrees from +1 to +15, and from -10 to -1), but
it is large compared to the boundary band's own width (~1.25 degrees).
Within the band, radius is a first-order effect; outside the band, it
is negligible.  This is why the (lambda, angle) two-coordinate system
achieves 94-97% accuracy in the bulk but only 70% overall: the 30%
residual is concentrated in the boundary band where radius matters.


Drift Clause

Drift in the boundary band does not accumulate over time as it does in
the lambda >> 1 regime.  At lambda << 1, collapse is instantaneous, and
the drift perturbation acts as a single-timestep radial velocity offset:
the effective radial component at the moment of collapse is
v_r = sin(gamma) + drift, computed once and applied once.

This single-shot character makes drift's effect in the boundary band
qualitatively different from its effect in the bulk.  In the bulk,
where |sin(gamma)| >> drift, the drift perturbation is a sub-leading
correction that changes nothing.  In the boundary band, where
|sin(gamma)| ~ drift, the perturbation is a leading-order contribution
that can flip the sign of v_r and thereby flip the mechanism identity.

The drift-rigidity decomposition confirms this structure.  At N >= 40,
bulk drift rigidity reaches 98-100%: not a single mechanism label
changes across drift values of 0 to 0.030 at angles beyond +/-1
degree.  Boundary-layer drift rigidity plateaus at 38-47%: more than
half of all boundary-layer cells change mechanism identity under drift
perturbation.  The plateau does not improve with increasing N because
it is not a lambda-dependent quantity -- it is a property of the
v_r ~ 0 condition, which persists at all N.

The specific drift value at which a boundary-layer cell flips depends
on the angle and the radius.  At gamma = -0.5 degrees, the nominal
v_r = -0.0087.  A drift of d = 0.010 produces v_r = +0.0013, flipping
the sign to inward.  But at r = 0.500 (the maximally symmetric radius),
this sign flip does not produce IC because the PBC geometry cancels the
inward bias.  At r = 0.150, the same drift does produce IC because the
PBC geometry amplifies it.  The drift-flip threshold is therefore
radius-dependent within the boundary band, and the observed 56% flip
rate is the average over the full radius distribution.

Drift sensitivity within the boundary band is bounded.  The maximum
drift tested (d = 0.030) has a radial component larger than
sin(1 degree) ~ 0.017, so it can overwhelm the radial velocity at
angles up to +/-1 degree.  But it cannot penetrate into the bulk: at
gamma = +2 degrees, sin(2) = 0.035, and even the maximum drift is
insufficient to flip the sign.  The boundary band's angular width is
therefore set by the ratio of the maximum relevant drift to the
sin(gamma) function: the band extends to roughly the angle where
sin(gamma) equals the drift scale, which is approximately +/-1 degree
for the tested drift range.


Boundary-Layer Clause

The boundary band is a distinct dynamical regime, not merely the
overlap of two bulk sectors.  Within the band, mechanism identity is
probabilistic in the following precise sense: at any fixed angle within
the band, the mechanism outcome varies across the (radius, drift)
parameter space without a single-valued prediction rule.  The IC
fraction at gamma = -0.5 degrees ranges from 27% (N = 12) to 60%
(N = 32) to 46% (N = 128), depending on which radii and drifts are
sampled.  No deterministic function of (lambda, angle) alone can
predict the outcome at this angle because the outcome depends on
radius and PBC geometry, which are not captured by these two
coordinates.

The boundary band's probabilistic character is structural, not
stochastic.  Each individual cell at a given (N, radius, angle, drift)
is fully deterministic -- the simulation produces the same mechanism
every time it is run.  But the mapping from (N, angle) to mechanism
is multi-valued across radii, and this multi-valuedness is the
boundary band's defining feature.  It is the geometric expression of
the PBC box's broken symmetry: a ring of particles on an infinite
plane would have a sharp IC boundary at exactly gamma = 0 (where the
radial component changes sign), but the PBC box introduces
radius-dependent perturbations that smear this sharp boundary into a
band.

Drift rigidity within the band saturates at approximately 40%.  This
value is not arbitrary; it reflects the fraction of (radius, angle)
pairs in the band where the nominal v_r is large enough to resist even
the strongest tested drift.  At the band's edges (gamma near +/-1
degree), |sin(gamma)| ~ 0.017, which is comparable to the mid-range
drift scale, so roughly half of drift perturbations overwhelm it.  At
the band's center (gamma near 0), sin(gamma) ~ 0, and every nonzero
drift flips the mechanism, so rigidity drops toward zero.  The 40%
plateau is the radius-angle-averaged result of this gradient.


Temporal Clause

The boundary band carries no temporal signature in the lambda << 1
regime.  At N >= 48, collapse time chi is at the measurement floor
(0.01) for 99.9% of all cells regardless of whether they lie in the
boundary band, the inward sector, or the outward sector.  The mechanism
distinction between IC and OPBC persists, but both collapse within a
single timestep.

This temporal invisibility is why the boundary band escaped detection
through Law VIII's complexity-reduction analysis.  The five descriptive
manifolds unified by Law VIII -- mechanism, boundary, temporal, atlas,
and drift -- are all trivial in the lambda << 1 regime: the boundary
is flat, the temporal landscape is at floor, the atlas is uniform, and
the drift response is rigid.  The mechanism manifold alone carries
non-trivial structure at large N, and within the mechanism manifold,
only the boundary band deviates from the two-coordinate prediction.
The band is invisible to curvature, torsion, manifold distance, and
every geometric diagnostic that operates on the temporal or boundary
layers.  It is visible only to the mechanism layer -- specifically, to
the IC fraction as a function of angle at fixed N.

In the lambda >> 1 regime (N = 4), the boundary band is obscured by
the folded boundary geometry of Law I.  The phase boundary at N = 4
has 3 folds, 8 cusps, and 5 topological holes, and the angle partition
is not a clean predictor at any angle.  The boundary band exists in
principle -- there is still an angular region where IC and non-IC
sectors meet -- but its structure is buried beneath the trajectory
complexity of the folded manifold.  Law XI's boundary band is a
clean, measurable feature only in the lambda <= 1 regime (N >= 12).

In the lambda ~ 1 resonance (N = 8), the boundary band hosts the
OL-wandering population (21% of all N = 8 cells).  The wandering
orbits are near-tangential trajectories that execute extended loops
before collapsing -- a phenomenon that requires both the boundary
band's small v_r and the lambda ~ 1 regime's marginal trajectory
lifetime.  The resonance is the temporal amplification of boundary-band
physics: the band provides the near-tangential launch directions, and
lambda ~ 1 provides the time for those directions to produce observable
wandering.  At lambda << 1, the same launch directions produce
OL-stalled instead of OL-wandering, because collapse is too fast for
any wandering to develop.


Exceptions and Degeneracies

The maximally symmetric radius (r = 0.500) is the primary exception
to the boundary band's generic structure.  At this radius, the ring
circumradius equals half the box side, producing a configuration where
all particles are equidistant from the nearest PBC walls.  The
inward geometric bias that normally shifts the IC boundary to negative
angles vanishes, and the effective boundary moves above +1 degree.
This is not a breakdown of the boundary band but an extreme of its
radius modulation: the band still exists, but its center is displaced
far enough that the standard boundary-layer angles (-1 to +1 degree)
sample only the outward side of the transition.

The N = 4 folded regime overrides the boundary band with trajectory
complexity.  At lambda >> 1, mechanism identity is determined by the
full trajectory from launch to collapse, not by the instantaneous
velocity decomposition.  The angle partition and its boundary band
are lambda << 1 phenomena in the sense that they are clean and
measurable only when trajectories are short enough that the initial
velocity determines the outcome.  At N = 4, the boundary band is
present but inaccessible to the simple geometric description of
Law XI.

The 10 total sub-types observed across the full N = 4 to 128 range
(IC-direct, IC-bounced, OPBC-single, OPBC-glancing, OPBC-winding,
OL-stalled, OL-wandering, CR-diagonal, DE-tangent, DE-frozen) are
all consistent with the boundary band's three-sector geometry.  No
sub-type appears exclusively within the boundary band except
OL-stalled and OL-wandering, which are the tangential-launch
mechanisms that require v_r ~ 0.  The boundary band does not generate
new mechanism types; it generates ambiguity between the existing types
by placing cells in the transition zone between sectors.

The boundary band's width does not shrink to zero at any tested N.
At N = 128, the transition from 32% IC (at -0.75 degrees) to 93% IC
(at +0.25 degrees) spans 1 degree -- the same width as at N = 48.
This non-convergence is a structural consequence of the PBC geometry:
the box-induced perturbations that smear the boundary are independent
of N and lambda, so no amount of particle-count increase can sharpen
the band to a line.  The band is permanent.


Closing Synthesis

Law XI completes the structural description of the ED-Arch manifold
by formalizing the last feature that resists the two-coordinate
reduction.

Law IX showed that lambda governs the temporal regime: when collapse
happens.  Law X showed that angle governs the mechanism identity: how
collapse happens.  Law XI shows that the transition between the two
mechanism sectors is not a line but a band, and that band's geometry
is modulated by the ring-to-box radius in a systematic way.  The
manifold's generative system is (lambda, angle, radius), but the three
coordinates are not equal.  Lambda is the primary coordinate: it
determines whether the manifold is folded, resonant, or planar.  Angle
is the secondary coordinate: it determines which mechanism sector each
cell occupies.  Radius is the tertiary coordinate: it modulates the
boundary between sectors by up to +/-0.25 degrees and determines
which sub-mechanism variant (direct vs. bounced, single vs. glancing)
appears within each sector.

The boundary band is the last surviving geometric feature in the
asymptotic hyperplane.  When lambda << 1 and every other observable
has converged to its trivial limit -- curvature zero, torsion zero,
chi at floor, drift rigidity at ceiling -- the boundary band still
carries structure: a radius-dependent center, a finite width, a 40%
drift-rigidity floor, and a mixed IC/OPBC composition that varies
from cell to cell.  Every residual deviation from the two-coordinate
prediction catalogued in Law X -- the 30% mechanism-prediction
residual, the non-convergence of the boundary angle, the drift-rigidity
ceiling at 83% -- originates in the boundary band.  The band is the
irreducible geometric cost of wrapping a ring around a periodic box.

The boundary band cannot be eliminated.  It is not a finite-N
artifact, not a grid-resolution limitation, and not a dynamical
transient.  It is a geometric consequence of the velocity
decomposition's tangent line intersecting the PBC box's broken
symmetry.  On an infinite plane without periodic boundaries, the
IC/non-IC transition would be a sharp line at gamma = 0.  The PBC box
smears that line into a band, and the band's geometry depends on the
ring-to-box ratio because that ratio determines how the PBC images
perturb the effective radial velocity.  As long as the simulation
domain is periodic, the boundary band exists.

The ED-Arch manifold is generated by three coordinates: lambda tells
it when to collapse, angle tells it how, and radius tells it where
the boundary between "how" and "how else" lies.
