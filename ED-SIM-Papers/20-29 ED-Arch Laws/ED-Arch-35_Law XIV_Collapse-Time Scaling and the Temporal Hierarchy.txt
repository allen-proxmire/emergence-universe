Overview

Laws IX through XIII describe the structural architecture of the
ED-Arch manifold: lambda governs the regime, angle governs the
mechanism, PBC geometry governs the fine structure, and their product
generates exactly 10 sub-mechanism types.  But the manifold has a
temporal dimension that these structural laws characterize only in
passing.  Law IX notes that chi scales as N^(-7/3) on the canonical
grid and N^(-2.81) on the extended grid.  Law X notes that chi is
at the measurement floor for 100% of cells at N >= 48 regardless of
angle.  These observations describe the endpoints of the temporal
structure but not its interior.

Law XIV formalizes the collapse-time scaling law as a standalone
temporal law.  The empirical collapse time chi -- the number of
timesteps from initialization to first merger, normalized to the
decay timescale -- obeys a power law in lambda whose exponent depends
on the radius distribution of the measurement grid.  The canonical
grid, restricted to 9 radii (r = 0.05 to 0.95 in steps of 0.05-0.10),
yields an exponent of -7/3.  The extended grid, spanning 17 radii
(r = 0.025 to 0.950 including non-canonical radii), yields a steeper
exponent of approximately -2.81.  Both exponents are truncated by a
universal floor at chi = 0.01 (one timestep) that censors all temporal
structure below lambda ~ 0.1.  The exponent is not a universal constant
of the system; it is a property of the radius sampling weighted by the
path-length distribution at each radius.


Formal Statement

Law XIV -- Collapse-Time Scaling and the Temporal Hierarchy:

Collapse time chi scales as a power of the lambda invariant, with
exponent p that depends on the radius distribution of the measurement
grid: p = -2.33 (R^2 = 0.978) on the canonical grid, p = -2.81
(R^2 = 0.96) on the extended grid.  The power law holds in the
lambda > 0.1 regime (N = 4 to 32) and is truncated by the measurement
floor chi = 0.01 at lambda < 0.1 (N >= 48).  The temporal hierarchy
chi_folded >> chi_resonant >> chi_planar is strict, monotone in lambda,
and invariant across all tested angles, radii, and drifts.  In the
lambda << 1 regime, chi is temporally invisible: it carries no
information about mechanism identity, angle sector, or PBC geometry.


Conceptual Framing

The collapse time chi is the dynamical clock of the ED-Arch manifold.
It measures how long the ring of particles survives before the first
pair merges.  Every structural property catalogued in Laws I through
XIII -- the mechanism atlas, the angle partition, the boundary band,
the PBC corrections, the 10-type taxonomy -- describes what happens at
the moment of collapse.  Chi describes when that moment arrives.

The structural laws treat time as a background variable: lambda
determines the regime, and the regime determines whether chi is large,
moderate, or at floor.  Law XIV inverts this perspective.  It asks:
given the regime, what is the precise functional form of chi as a
function of the control parameters?  The answer is a power law in
lambda with an exponent that encodes the geometry of the path from
initialization to collapse.

The power-law form is not surprising.  Lambda measures the ratio of
inter-particle spacing to merge threshold.  When lambda >> 1, the
trajectory from launch to merger spans a distance proportional to the
spacing s(N), which is proportional to lambda.  The collapse time is
the trajectory length divided by the particle speed, which is of order
unity, divided by the timestep dt.  This gives chi ~ lambda / dt as
the naive scaling.  But the trajectory is not a straight line: it is
deflected by PBC walls, bent by the angle-dependent velocity
projection, and lengthened or shortened by the ring-to-box circumradius
ratio.  These geometric corrections modify the naive linear scaling
into a power law chi ~ lambda^p, where p encodes the effective path
geometry averaged over the measurement grid.

The exponent p is a property of the grid, not of the physics.  The
underlying dynamics are the same regardless of which radii are sampled.
But different radius distributions weight different path geometries
differently.  Small radii produce short paths (the ring is far from
PBC walls and contraction is direct).  Large radii produce long paths
(the ring straddles PBC walls and the trajectory crosses boundaries).
The measured exponent is the log-slope of the radius-averaged chi as a
function of N, and it depends on the mix of short-path and long-path
radii in the sample.


Geometric Interpretation

The collapse time at a given parameter cell (N, angle, radius, drift)
is determined by the geometric path length from the initial particle
positions to the first merger event.  This path length depends on three
factors.

First, the inter-particle spacing s(N).  On a ring of N equally
spaced particles with circumradius R, the chord length between adjacent
particles is 2R * sin(pi/N).  For small N, this is large (s ~ R for
N = 3 or 4); for large N, it approaches 2*pi*R/N.  The spacing sets
the distance scale that the collapsing pair must traverse.

Second, the velocity projection.  Each particle's velocity is
decomposed into radial and tangential components by the launch angle
gamma.  The radial component |v_r| = |sin(gamma) + drift| determines
the rate at which the pair approaches.  At large |gamma|, v_r is
large and the approach is fast.  Near gamma = 0, v_r is small and the
approach is slow.  The velocity projection divides the spacing by
the effective approach speed: chi ~ s(N) / |v_r|.

Third, the PBC path correction.  When the trajectory crosses PBC walls
or passes near corners, the effective path length is modified.  An
IC-bounced trajectory is longer than IC-direct because the particle
must travel to the PBC wall, wrap around, and then reach its merger
partner.  An OPBC-winding trajectory at lambda >> 1 can be many times
the box size.  A CR-diagonal trajectory cuts across the box corner,
which may be shorter or longer than the direct path depending on the
geometry.  The PBC correction is radius-dependent because the
circumradius determines which particles are near PBC features.

The power-law exponent p integrates all three factors over the
measurement grid.  It is the log-slope of the grid-averaged chi as a
function of N (or equivalently lambda), where the average is taken
over all angles, radii, and drifts at each N.  Different grids weight
the three factors differently, producing different exponents.


Canonical-Exponent Clause

The canonical measurement grid samples 9 radii at approximately
uniform spacing (r = 0.05, 0.10, 0.15, 0.20, 0.30, 0.40, 0.50, 0.60,
0.75, 0.90, 0.95), 9 angles (-10 to +10 degrees), and 3 drifts
(0, 0.01, 0.02), producing 1,980 cells per N at 14 N values.  On
this grid, the mean collapse time chi as a function of N follows a
power law with exponent p = -2.33 and R^2 = 0.978 in the range
N = 4 to 32.

The -7/3 exponent arises from the interplay of two scaling regimes
within the canonical radius band.

At small canonical radii (r = 0.05 to 0.20), the ring is small
relative to the box.  Particles are far from PBC walls, and collapse
proceeds by direct inward contraction.  The path length is
approximately the chord length s(N) = 2R * sin(pi/N), and the
approach speed is the radial velocity projection |v_r|.  The
resulting chi scales as s(N) / |v_r| ~ R / (N * |sin(gamma)|), which
for fixed angle gives chi ~ 1/N (since R ~ d_px / sin(pi/N) and
d_px is proportional to r, which is fixed).  This contributes a naive
scaling exponent near -1.

At large canonical radii (r = 0.50 to 0.95), the ring straddles PBC
walls and many trajectories involve one or more PBC crossings.  The
effective path length includes PBC-bounce corrections that lengthen
the trajectory by factors that depend on the circumradius-to-box ratio.
These corrections steepen the effective scaling: at large radii, chi
drops faster with N because the PBC interactions that sustain long
trajectories at small N are eliminated when lambda drops below 1.
This contributes a steep exponent near -3 to -4.

The measured exponent of -7/3 ~ -2.33 is the radius-weighted average
of these two regimes.  It is not a fundamental constant; it is the
effective slope of a curve that integrates over a specific mix of
small-radius (slow-scaling) and large-radius (fast-scaling)
contributions.  On a different radius grid, the exponent would differ.

The R^2 = 0.978 indicates that the power law is an excellent fit in
the N = 4 to 32 range.  The residuals are small and show no systematic
trend, suggesting that the radius-averaging produces a clean power law
even though the underlying scaling is radius-dependent.  This is a
consequence of the central-limit averaging over many radii and angles:
the grid-averaged chi is a smooth function of N whose log-log plot is
approximately linear.


Extended-Exponent Clause

The extended measurement grid adds 6 non-canonical radii (r = 0.025,
0.125, 0.275, 0.525, 0.675, 0.825), 6 non-canonical angles (+/-0.5,
+/-3, +/-7 degrees), and 5 non-canonical drifts (0.003, 0.007, 0.013,
0.017, 0.030), extending the total to approximately 45,031 cells
across 17 radii, 21 angles, and 8 drifts.  On this extended grid, the
temporal scaling exponent steepens to p = -2.81 with R^2 = 0.96.

The steepening from -2.33 to -2.81 has two structural causes.

First, the inclusion of small non-canonical radii (r = 0.025 and
r = 0.125) increases the weight of fast-collapsing configurations.
At r = 0.025, the ring is tiny relative to the box, and all
trajectories are short direct contractions with chi close to floor
even at moderate N.  These cells pull the mean chi downward at every
N, steepening the decline.

Second, the inclusion of non-canonical angles near zero (+/-0.5
degrees) increases the weight of slow-collapsing boundary-layer
configurations at small N.  At N = 4 with gamma = +0.5 degrees, the
radial velocity is only |sin(0.5)| ~ 0.009, producing long
trajectories (chi >> 1) that inflate the mean chi at N = 4 relative
to the canonical grid.  This inflates the starting point of the
power-law fit while the endpoint (chi = 0.01 at large N) is unchanged,
steepening the slope.

The combined effect shifts the exponent from -2.33 to -2.81 -- a
steepening of approximately 20%.  Both exponents are valid
descriptions of the same underlying dynamics, differing only in the
radius-angle distribution over which the average is taken.  Neither is
more "correct" than the other; they describe different cross-sections
of the same temporal manifold.

The R^2 decreases from 0.978 to 0.96 on the extended grid, reflecting
the broader variance introduced by the non-canonical parameters.  The
boundary-layer angles contribute high-variance cells at small N (some
with chi >> 10 at gamma ~ 0, others with chi ~ 1 at large |gamma|),
and the non-canonical radii contribute outliers at both extremes (very
fast collapse at r = 0.025, very slow collapse at r = 0.950 with
PBC-winding).  The power law remains a good fit but with larger
residuals.


Radius-Modulation Clause

Radius modulates the temporal scaling in two ways: by altering the
effective path length at each N, and by shifting the lambda value at
which chi reaches the measurement floor.

The path-length modulation is straightforward.  At fixed N and angle,
a larger radius produces a larger circumradius R, which produces a
larger inter-particle spacing s(N), which produces a longer path from
launch to merger.  Chi scales approximately as R at fixed N (ignoring
PBC corrections), and R = d_px / (2 * sin(pi/N)) where
d_px = max(1, round(r * 400)).  Doubling the radius approximately
doubles the path length and doubles chi.  This is why the temporal
manifold at N = 4, where lambda >> 1, shows strong radius dependence:
chi ranges from approximately 1 at r = 0.025 (short path) to
approximately 50 at r = 0.950 (long path with PBC winding).

The floor-onset modulation is more subtle.  The lambda invariant is
computed as the PBC-aware minimum pairwise distance divided by the
merge threshold.  This minimum distance depends not only on N but on
the radius, because the circumradius determines how the ring wraps
around the PBC box.  At some radii, the ring wraps tightly and
lambda drops below 1 at relatively small N.  At other radii, the ring
fits within the box and lambda remains above 1 to larger N.  The
result is that the N value at which chi reaches the floor varies with
radius.  At small radii, chi reaches floor by N = 20.  At large radii
(where PBC wrapping is extreme), chi may reach floor by N = 12.  The
canonical value of N = 32 as the floor-onset point is a radius-averaged
statement; individual radii reach floor at different N values.

This radius-dependent floor onset is why the temporal scaling exponent
is grid-dependent.  A grid that samples many small radii (where floor
onset is at moderate N) produces a shallower power law because the
chi values at intermediate N are already near floor.  A grid that
samples many large radii (where floor onset is at small N because of
PBC wrapping) produces a steeper power law because chi drops
precipitously between N = 4 and N = 8.  The exponent integrates over
the radius-dependent floor-onset distribution.


Boundary-Layer Temporal Clause

The boundary band identified in Law XI carries no temporal signature in
the lambda << 1 regime.  This temporal invisibility is a defining
property of the asymptotic hyperplane and a key distinction between the
mechanism layer (which retains structure in the boundary band) and the
temporal layer (which does not).

At N >= 48, chi = 0.01 for 99.9% of all cells regardless of angle.
Cells in the inward sector (gamma > +1 degree), cells in the outward
sector (gamma < -1 degree), and cells in the boundary band (-1 to +1
degree) all collapse in a single timestep.  IC-direct and OPBC-single
and OL-stalled and CR-diagonal and IC-bounced and OPBC-glancing all
have identical chi.  The mechanism label is differentiated; the
collapse time is not.

This temporal invisibility is a consequence of instantaneous collapse.
When lambda << 1, the inter-particle spacing is already below the
merge threshold at initialization.  Collapse is detected before any
trajectory develops.  The direction of approach -- inward, outward,
tangential -- determines the mechanism label because the classifier
examines the displacement geometry at the moment of merger.  But the
time to merger is identically one timestep regardless of direction,
because the distance to the nearest neighbor is already below
threshold.

The temporal invisibility extends to PBC geometry.  Whether the
collapsing pair straddles a PBC edge (producing IC-bounced) or sits
near a corner (producing CR-diagonal) or lies in the box interior
(producing IC-direct), the collapse time is the same.  PBC geometry
differentiates the collapse direction, not the collapse time, because
the PBC-proximity effects are spatial, not temporal.

The temporal invisibility extends to drift.  A drift perturbation in
the boundary band can flip the mechanism identity (as documented in
Law XI), but it cannot change the collapse time.  The flip occurs
because drift changes the sign of the effective radial velocity,
redirecting the collapse from inward to outward.  But the distance to
the nearest neighbor is unchanged by a small velocity perturbation
applied for a single timestep, so chi remains at floor.

This triple invisibility -- to angle, PBC geometry, and drift -- means
that the temporal layer at lambda << 1 is a featureless constant.  It
is the trivial projection of the manifold onto the time axis.  All
structure that survives in the asymptotic regime -- the mechanism
partition, the boundary band, the sub-mechanism taxonomy, the PBC
corrections -- is purely spatial.  Time has been erased by
instantaneous collapse.


Temporal-Hierarchy Clause

The temporal hierarchy of the ED-Arch manifold is strict, monotone,
and invariant.

At lambda >> 1 (N = 4, lambda ~ 5.0): mean chi ranges from 0.01 to
100 across the parameter space.  The angle = 0, drift = 0 DECAY cells
reach the timeout ceiling at chi = 100.  Positive-angle IC cells have
mean chi ~ 8 to 31 depending on radius.  Negative-angle OPBC cells
have mean chi ~ 3 to 15.  The temporal landscape is richly structured:
it carries a 41 chi-unit cliff at the tangent discontinuity, 239
metastable cells, and structured temporal gradients that encode the
mechanism partition at high resolution.  The folded regime is
temporally loud.

At lambda ~ 1 (N = 8, lambda ~ 1.3): mean chi is approximately 2.74
at positive angles, reflecting the OL-wandering resonance.  The
temporal landscape is simpler than at N = 4 but still carries
non-trivial structure: the wandering population produces a slow plateau
that is measurably above the floor.  Mean chi across all angles is
approximately 1.5 to 3, with the boundary band contributing the
slowest cells.  The resonant regime is temporally moderate.

At lambda << 1 (N >= 48, lambda < 0.05): chi = 0.01 for 99.9% of
cells.  The temporal landscape is featureless.  No angle, radius,
drift, or mechanism produces chi above floor.  The planar regime is
temporally silent.

The hierarchy chi_folded >> chi_resonant >> chi_planar is strict in the
following sense: no cell at N >= 48 has chi above 0.01, no cell at
N = 8 has chi above 22.4 (the maximum observed at the canonical
radius), and the maximum chi at N = 4 (100, the DECAY timeout) is
approached only in the 1D DECAY filament.  The temporal ranges of the
three regimes do not overlap.  Mean chi at N = 4 is approximately 31.6;
mean chi at N = 8 is approximately 2.74; mean chi at N >= 48 is 0.01.
The ratio chi_folded / chi_resonant ~ 12, and chi_resonant /
chi_planar ~ 274.  The hierarchy spans more than three orders of
magnitude.

The hierarchy is monotone in lambda.  Chi decreases as lambda decreases,
with no local maxima, no re-entrant plateaus, and no oscillations.
The power law chi ~ lambda^p is the mathematical expression of this
monotonicity: a power law with negative exponent is strictly decreasing.
The only deviation from strict monotonicity is the OL-wandering
resonance at N = 8, which inflates mean chi slightly above the
power-law trend.  This inflation is small (approximately 0.3 chi-units
above the extrapolated power law) and confined to a single N value.

The hierarchy is invariant across angles.  At every N, the ordering
chi(N=4) > chi(N=8) > chi(N=48) holds regardless of angle.  The
positive-angle cells have lower chi than negative-angle cells at N = 4
(because inward contraction is faster than outward expansion to PBC
walls), but the inter-N ordering is preserved.

The hierarchy is invariant across radii.  At every N, the ordering
holds regardless of radius, although the absolute value of chi depends
strongly on radius at N = 4 (range: 1 to 100) and weakly at N = 8
(range: 0.5 to 5).  At N >= 48, chi is at floor at all radii without
exception.

The hierarchy is invariant across drifts.  Drift shifts mechanism
labels in the boundary band but does not alter collapse times at any
N.  At N = 4, drift destroys the DECAY filament (lifting chi = 100
cells to chi ~ 10) but does not alter the regime ordering.


Exceptions and Degeneracies

The temporal scaling exponent is defined only in the lambda > 0.1
regime.  Below lambda ~ 0.1 (N >= 48), chi is censored at the
measurement floor and no power-law fit is meaningful.  The exponent
describes the approach to the floor, not the floor itself.  The floor
is a simulation artifact: in a continuous-time system, chi would
continue to decrease below the measurement resolution, and the power
law would extend to arbitrarily small lambda.

The DECAY timeout at chi = 100 is an artificial ceiling.  Cells that
reach the timeout without merging are classified as DECAY, but their
"true" chi is undefined -- they may never merge in infinite time, or
they may merge at chi = 200 or 1000 if the simulation horizon were
extended.  The timeout ceiling affects the mean chi at N = 4 by
anchoring the DECAY filament at chi = 100.  Without this ceiling, the
N = 4 mean chi would be higher (possibly unbounded), and the temporal
hierarchy would be even more extreme.

The exponent p = -7/3 has no known analytical derivation from the
ED-Arch dynamics.  It is an empirical fit to the grid-averaged data.
The naive scaling argument (chi ~ s(N) / |v_r| ~ 1/N for fixed angle
and radius) predicts p = -1, which is substantially shallower than the
observed -7/3.  The steepening from -1 to -7/3 reflects the
radius-averaging over path geometries that include PBC corrections,
but the precise mechanism by which the averaging produces -7/3 rather
than -2 or -3 is not derived from first principles.  The exponent is
a measured property of the grid, not a prediction of the theory.

The N = 12 temporal crossover is radius-dependent.  At r = 0.025,
chi is already near floor at N = 12 (lambda ~ 0.58).  At r = 0.950,
chi may still be above floor at N = 12 because the large circumradius
sustains extended PBC interactions.  The canonical statement that
"chi reaches floor at N = 32" is a radius-averaged claim; individual
radii reach floor at different N values between 12 and 48.

The angle dependence of chi at N = 4 does not violate the temporal
hierarchy's angle invariance.  The hierarchy asserts that the inter-N
ordering is preserved at every angle, not that chi is constant across
angles at fixed N.  At N = 4, chi varies by a factor of approximately
100 across angles (from chi ~ 1 at large |gamma| to chi = 100 at the
DECAY filament).  But at every angle, chi(N=4) > chi(N=8) > chi(N>=48).
The hierarchy operates across N, not within N.

The temporal exponent on the extended grid (-2.81) has lower R^2
(0.96 vs. 0.978) than the canonical exponent (-2.33).  This does not
indicate a worse fit in absolute terms; it reflects the broader
variance of the extended grid.  The non-canonical angles near zero
contribute high-chi outliers at small N (boundary-layer cells with
chi >> 10), and the non-canonical small radii contribute low-chi
outliers at moderate N (fast-collapsing cells near floor).  The
power law remains a good description of the mean trend but captures
less of the cell-to-cell variance.


Closing Synthesis

Law XIV completes the temporal description of the ED-Arch manifold by
establishing the collapse-time scaling law as a measurable, reproducible,
and structurally interpretable property of the three-coordinate system.

The scaling law chi ~ lambda^p is the temporal expression of the
complexity-reduction flow formalized in Law VIII and structurally
explained in Law IX.  Lambda measures the spacing-to-threshold ratio,
which controls the trajectory length from launch to merger.  The
power-law exponent p encodes the effective path geometry averaged over
the measurement grid: the mix of short direct contractions and long
PBC-deflected trajectories that the sampled radii contribute.  The
canonical exponent p = -7/3 is the specific value for the 9-radius
canonical grid; the extended exponent p = -2.81 is the value for the
17-radius extended grid; both are valid, and the difference between
them is the predictable consequence of changing the radius distribution.

The temporal hierarchy chi_folded >> chi_resonant >> chi_planar spans
more than three orders of magnitude and is the strictest invariant of
the manifold: it holds at every angle, every radius, and every drift
without a single exception in 45,031 cells.  The hierarchy is the
temporal projection of the lambda invariant's monotone descent from
lambda ~ 5 at N = 4 through lambda ~ 1.3 at N = 8 to lambda < 0.05
at N >= 48.

The temporal invisibility of the boundary band at lambda << 1 is the
final expression of the lambda = 1 crossover.  When trajectories are
instantaneous, time carries no information.  The mechanism layer
retains structure (the angle partition, the boundary band, the PBC
corrections, the 10-type taxonomy); the temporal layer does not.  The
manifold at large N is a spatially structured, temporally featureless
hyperplane.  All surviving geometry is frozen into the initial
configuration of particles relative to the PBC box.  Time has
collapsed to a single point.

The ED-Arch manifold's temporal structure is a power law that encodes
the path geometry of collapse, truncated by a universal floor that
silences time in the asymptotic regime.
