Overview

Laws IX through XI describe the ED-Arch manifold as generated by three
coordinates -- lambda for temporal regime, angle for mechanism identity,
and radius for boundary-band modulation -- but they treat PBC geometry
as a local correction visible only in the boundary band.  The full
consolidation data reveals a stronger claim.  PBC geometry is not
confined to the boundary band.  It operates across the entire manifold:
in the inward sector, where it splits IC-direct from IC-bounced; in the
outward sector, where it sustains CR-diagonal and OPBC-glancing as
permanent minorities; and in the boundary band, where it determines
which side of the IC/non-IC transition each cell occupies.  These three
effects -- edge proximity, corner proximity, and symmetry breaking --
are distinct geometric mechanisms, each with its own radius dependence,
each persistent across all N, and each irreducible to lambda or angle.

Law XII promotes PBC geometry from a boundary-band correction (Law XI)
to a manifold-wide structural determinant.  It formalizes the
observation that every departure from the ideal two-coordinate
prediction -- every sub-mechanism variant, every mechanism minority in
the bulk, every mixed-zone cell in the boundary band -- traces back to
the interaction between the ring's circumradius and the periodic box's
edge-and-corner geometry.  PBC geometry is the third coordinate not
because it controls the regime or the mechanism sector (lambda and angle
do that) but because it determines the fine structure within each
sector and the transition geometry between sectors.


Formal Statement

Law XII -- PBC Geometry and the Three-Coordinate Generative Hierarchy:

Periodic boundary geometry introduces persistent directional biases and
collapse pathways that cannot be removed by increasing N, decreasing
lambda, or refining the angular partition.  Three PBC-driven effects --
edge proximity, corner proximity, and symmetry breaking -- generate the
sub-mechanism taxonomy, sustain mechanism minorities in the bulk
sectors, and modulate the boundary band.  The ED-Arch manifold is
generated by a three-coordinate hierarchy: lambda (primary) sets the
temporal regime, angle (secondary) selects the mechanism sector, and
PBC geometry mediated by radius (tertiary) determines the fine
structure within each sector and the transition geometry between them.


Conceptual Framing

On an infinite plane without periodic boundaries, the ED-Arch manifold
would be trivially two-dimensional.  Lambda would set the collapse
timescale, angle would set the mechanism, and every cell in the inward
sector would produce a single mechanism (call it IC), every cell in the
outward sector would produce a single mechanism (call it OPBC), and the
boundary between them would be a sharp line at gamma = 0.  There would
be no sub-types, no mechanism minorities, no mixed zones, and no radius
dependence.  The manifold would be a step function in angle, modulated
in time by lambda.

The periodic box destroys this simplicity.  By identifying opposite
edges of the simulation domain, PBC creates a topology in which every
particle has an infinite lattice of images.  The nearest image of a
given particle may lie inward, outward, or diagonally, depending on
where the particle sits relative to the box edges and corners.  When
the radial velocity component v_r is large (deep in the inward or
outward sector), the image geometry is a sub-leading correction: the
particle's trajectory is dominated by v_r and the image perturbation
changes only the sub-mechanism label (direct vs. bounced, single vs.
glancing).  When v_r is small (in the boundary band), the image
geometry becomes a leading-order effect: it can flip the mechanism
identity entirely.

This is why PBC geometry operates at two scales simultaneously.  In
the bulk, it is a fine-structure effect that generates the 6 sub-type
taxonomy from the 3 angle-protected cores.  In the boundary band, it
is a coarse-structure effect that determines the mechanism identity
itself.  Law XI identified the boundary-band role; Law XII identifies
both roles and unifies them as expressions of a single geometric cause:
the ring-to-box radius ratio determining which PBC images are nearest
to each particle at the moment of collapse.


Geometric Interpretation

The three PBC-driven effects are distinct geometric mechanisms, each
with a clear physical origin and a measurable manifestation in the
sub-mechanism data.

Edge proximity is the interaction between a particle and the nearest
PBC wall.  When a particle lies within a distance comparable to the
merge threshold from a box edge, its nearest PBC image lies on the
opposite side of that edge, in the inward direction relative to the
ring center.  This image creates an effective inward bias in the
collapse geometry: even a particle launched outward may merge with the
inward image before it reaches the far side of the box.  Edge proximity
is the dominant PBC effect.  It is responsible for IC-bounced (7.7% of
all N >= 48 cells), which is IC-direct modified by one or more PBC
wall crossings during the inward contraction.  IC-bounced occurs
exclusively at radii where the circumradius places ring particles near
box edges, and its frequency oscillates with radius as different
particles sweep past the edge threshold.

Corner proximity is the interaction between a particle and the nearest
PBC corner.  At a box corner, two edges meet and two PBC images
converge along the diagonal direction.  A particle near a corner
experiences a diagonal pull that is neither purely inward nor purely
outward, creating a collapse pathway that cuts across the standard
angle partition.  Corner proximity generates CR-diagonal (4.7% of all
N >= 48 cells), the only mechanism that cannot be predicted from the
sign of v_r alone.  CR-diagonal appears at radii where the
circumradius-to-box ratio places particles near the 45-degree diagonal
of the PBC cell, and it appears at both positive and negative angles --
it is not confined to either sector.

Symmetry breaking is the global effect of PBC geometry on the
mechanism census.  On an infinite plane, the inward sector would be
100% IC and the outward sector would be 100% OPBC.  PBC breaks this
purity.  In the inward sector (gamma > +1 degree), the IC fraction
at N >= 20 is 94.5%, not 100%.  The missing 5.5% consists of
OPBC-single (3-4%), CR-diagonal (1-2%), and scattered OL cells --
all produced by radius-specific PBC geometries where the ring-to-box
ratio redirects the nominally inward trajectory through an edge or
corner interaction.  In the outward sector (gamma < -1 degree), the
non-IC fraction at N >= 20 is 96.7%, with the remaining 3.3% being
IC cells at radii where edge proximity overrides the outward radial
component.  These mechanism minorities are permanent features: they
do not shrink with increasing N because they are set by the
radius-PBC geometry, not by lambda.


Edge-Proximity Clause

Edge proximity is the most frequent PBC effect and the primary source
of sub-mechanism diversity in the inward sector.

When a ring of N particles is initialized with circumradius R in a
unit PBC box centered at (0.5, 0.5), each particle's distance to the
nearest box edge is d_edge = 0.5 - max(|x - 0.5|, |y - 0.5|) for the
nearest axis-aligned edge.  When d_edge is comparable to or smaller
than the merge threshold (0.05875 in engine units), the particle's
nearest PBC image lies across that edge, at a distance of approximately
2 * d_edge -- which may be less than the distance to the nearest ring
neighbor.  Collapse then occurs through the PBC image rather than
through the direct inward path.

This produces IC-bounced when the net trajectory is still inward
(the particle contracts toward the center but crosses a PBC wall in
the process) and OPBC-single when the trajectory is outward (the
particle expands and merges with the image across the nearest edge).
The distinction between these outcomes depends on which is closer:
the nearest ring neighbor along the inward direction, or the nearest
PBC image across the edge.  This is a purely geometric calculation
that depends on R, N, and the particle's angular position on the
ring -- all of which are determined by the radius parameter.

Edge proximity explains why IC-bounced oscillates with N.  As N
increases at fixed radius, the circumradius R = d_px / (2 * sin(pi/N))
changes, and different particles sweep past the edge-proximity
threshold.  At N = 32, IC-bounced reaches 7.7% because the
circumradius at several tested radii places particles within the
edge-proximity zone.  At N = 48, it drops to 5.1% as the circumradius
shifts.  At N = 56, it rises to 8.4%.  These oscillations are not
noise; they are the deterministic consequence of the ring-to-box
geometry cycling through edge-proximity resonances.


Corner-Proximity Clause

Corner proximity is the second PBC effect and the sole source of
the CR-diagonal mechanism.

At a box corner, two PBC walls meet and a particle has images in
three adjacent PBC cells: across the horizontal edge, across the
vertical edge, and diagonally across the corner.  The diagonal image
is at distance sqrt(dx^2 + dy^2) where dx and dy are the distances
to the two converging edges.  When this diagonal distance is less
than the distance to the nearest ring neighbor, collapse occurs
through the corner image, producing CR-diagonal.

CR-diagonal is geometrically distinct from both IC and OPBC mechanisms.
The collapse trajectory is neither inward toward the ring center nor
outward toward a single PBC wall; it cuts diagonally across the box
corner, producing a displacement vector at approximately 45 degrees
to both edges.  This is why CR-diagonal appears at both positive and
negative angles: the diagonal collapse pathway is orthogonal to the
radial direction and can be triggered regardless of whether the net
v_r is inward or outward, provided the particle is close enough to
the corner.

CR-diagonal accounts for 4.7% of all N >= 48 cells.  Its frequency
is stable across N from 48 to 128 (range: 4.2% to 5.1%) because the
fraction of radii that place particles near corners is a geometric
property of the radius distribution, not of N.  At specific radii,
notably those where the circumradius-to-box ratio is near 1/sqrt(2)
(placing particles on the box diagonal), CR-diagonal can reach 15-20%
of cells at that radius.  At other radii, it is absent entirely.
The overall 4.7% is the average over the tested radius distribution.

OPBC-glancing (2.1% at N >= 48) is a related but distinct corner
effect.  It occurs when a particle's outward trajectory passes near
but not through a box corner, producing a glancing PBC interaction
that deflects the trajectory without the full diagonal collapse of
CR-diagonal.  OPBC-glancing is confined to intermediate radii where
particles are near but not at the corner-proximity threshold, and
it appears only at negative angles where the outward trajectory is
directed toward the corner region.


Radius-Modulation Clause

Radius is the physical parameter through which PBC geometry enters
the generative hierarchy.  The ring-to-box circumradius ratio
R / box_size determines which particles are near edges, which are
near corners, and which are in the interior of the PBC cell.  This
ratio changes with the radius parameter r and with N (because
R = d_px / (2 * sin(pi/N)) and d_px = max(1, round(r * 400))).

The radius modulation has three measurable signatures:

First, the boundary-band position shifts by up to +/-0.25 degrees
across the 17 tested radii.  This was established by Law XI and is
confirmed here as a consequence of edge proximity: radii that place
particles near edges bias the effective v_r inward, shifting the IC
boundary to more negative angles.  The maximally symmetric radius
r = 0.500, where all particles are equidistant from edges, has zero
edge-proximity bias and the IC boundary is displaced above +1 degree.

Second, the sub-mechanism composition varies systematically with
radius.  At radii where edge proximity is strong, IC-bounced replaces
a fraction of IC-direct.  At radii where corner proximity is strong,
CR-diagonal replaces a fraction of IC-direct or OPBC-single.  At
radii where neither proximity effect is active, the taxonomy reduces
to the three angle-protected cores: IC-direct, OPBC-single, and
OL-stalled.  The 6-member taxonomy at N >= 48 is the union over all
tested radii of these radius-specific compositions.

Third, the mechanism minorities in the bulk sectors are entirely
radius-dependent.  The 5.5% non-IC fraction in the inward sector at
N >= 20 is not uniformly distributed across radii.  At some radii,
the inward sector is 100% IC-direct.  At others, it contains 10-15%
OPBC-single or CR-diagonal.  The overall 94.5% IC rate is the
radius-averaged value, and the 5.5% minority is the average over the
radii where PBC geometry overrides the angle partition.  If the
simulation were run at a single radius with no edge or corner
proximity, the inward-sector IC rate would be 100%.  The minority
is a PBC artifact mediated by radius.

Radius is a tertiary coordinate.  It does not control the regime
(lambda does that) or the mechanism sector (angle does that).  It
controls the fine structure: which sub-mechanism variant appears
within each sector, and where the boundary between sectors lies.
Its effect size is small in the bulk (+/-5% mechanism-fraction
modulation) and large in the boundary band (+/-25% IC-fraction
modulation, +/-0.25 degree boundary shift).  This asymmetry between
bulk and boundary-band effect sizes is the signature of a tertiary
coordinate: it matters only where the primary and secondary
coordinates leave the outcome undetermined.


Boundary-Band Interaction Clause

PBC geometry is the structural origin of the boundary band's three
defining properties: its finite width, its non-convergence with N,
and its drift sensitivity.

The boundary band has finite width because PBC geometry introduces
a range of effective radial velocity biases that vary with radius.
At gamma = -0.25 degrees, the nominal v_r is -0.0044.  But the
edge-proximity bias at a given radius may contribute +0.005 to the
effective v_r, flipping the net direction to inward and producing IC.
At a different radius, the edge-proximity bias is +0.002, insufficient
to flip the sign, and the mechanism remains OPBC.  The band's width
is set by the range of edge-proximity biases across the tested radii:
it extends from the most negative angle where any radius produces IC
(approximately -0.75 degrees) to the most positive angle where any
radius still produces non-IC (approximately +0.5 degrees).

The boundary band does not converge with N because the PBC-induced
biases are independent of lambda.  The edge-proximity bias depends
on d_edge, which is a function of R and the particle's angular
position on the ring.  As N increases, R changes (because the ring
diameter formula involves sin(pi/N)), and d_edge changes, but the
magnitude of the bias does not systematically decrease.  At N = 48,
the boundary band spans approximately 1.25 degrees.  At N = 128,
it spans the same 1.25 degrees.  The band is permanent because PBC
geometry is permanent.

Drift sensitivity in the boundary band is amplified by PBC geometry.
In the boundary band, the nominal v_r is small, and drift adds a
competing radial component.  But the drift-induced flip is not
determined by drift alone -- it is determined by the sum
v_r_eff = sin(gamma) + drift + PBC_bias(radius).  At radii where the
PBC bias reinforces the drift (both inward), the flip occurs at a
lower drift value.  At radii where the PBC bias opposes the drift
(PBC inward, drift outward), the flip requires a larger drift.  The
observed 56% flip rate in the boundary band is the average over all
radius-drift combinations, and the 4.9x ratio relative to the bulk
reflects the boundary band's unique condition of competing sub-leading
terms.  Drift sensitivity in the boundary band is a three-way
competition: v_r from the angle, the drift perturbation, and the PBC
bias from the radius.


Temporal Clause

PBC geometry carries no temporal signature in the lambda << 1 regime.
At N >= 48, collapse occurs within a single timestep regardless of
which PBC effect determines the mechanism.  IC-direct, IC-bounced,
OPBC-single, OPBC-glancing, CR-diagonal, and OL-stalled all have
chi = 0.01 (the measurement floor).  The PBC effects differentiate
the mechanism label but not the collapse time.

In the lambda >> 1 regime (N = 4), PBC geometry has a profound
temporal effect.  The rich sub-mechanism taxonomy at N = 4 -- 9
distinct types -- reflects the long trajectories that PBC geometry
deflects through multiple wall crossings, corner interactions, and
winding paths.  IC-bounced at N = 4 is a multi-step process where
the particle contracts inward, crosses a PBC wall, re-enters from
the opposite side, and then merges -- a trajectory that spans tens
of timesteps and produces chi >> 1.  OPBC-winding involves even
longer trajectories where the particle spirals outward through
multiple PBC cells before merging.  These extended trajectory types
are the temporal expression of PBC geometry at lambda >> 1, and they
are the reason the sub-mechanism taxonomy is 9 types at N = 4 but
only 6 at N >= 48.

The three sub-types lost in the lambda transition are DE-tangent,
DE-frozen, and OPBC-winding (which merges into OPBC-single at small
lambda).  DE-tangent and DE-frozen are DECAY variants confined to
N = 4 by Law VII.  OPBC-winding requires multiple PBC cell traversals,
which requires extended trajectories, which requires lambda >> 1.  At
lambda << 1, the outward trajectory is too short to wind; it merges
through the nearest single PBC wall, producing OPBC-single.  The
taxonomy contraction from 9 to 6 sub-types is the elimination of
trajectory-dependent PBC effects in favor of proximity-dependent PBC
effects.  At lambda >> 1, PBC geometry shapes the full trajectory.
At lambda << 1, PBC geometry shapes only the initial configuration --
which particles are nearest to which PBC features at t = 0.

In the lambda ~ 1 resonance (N = 8), PBC geometry contributes to the
OL-wandering population (21% of cells).  The wandering orbits are
near-tangential trajectories that interact repeatedly with PBC walls
during their extended lifetime.  Without PBC walls, these orbits
would drift to infinity; the periodic boundaries recycle them back
through the simulation domain, producing the characteristic wandering
pattern.  The OL-wandering excess at N = 8 is a PBC resonance: the
lambda ~ 1 timescale provides trajectories long enough to feel PBC
walls, and the boundary-band launch angles provide trajectories slow
enough to be redirected by them.


Exceptions and Degeneracies

The maximally symmetric radius r = 0.500 is the primary exception.
At this radius, the ring-to-box geometry produces a configuration
where all particles are equidistant from the nearest edges and the
nearest corners.  Edge-proximity and corner-proximity biases cancel
by symmetry, and the PBC effect on mechanism identity effectively
vanishes.  The IC boundary is pushed above +1 degree (no IC appears
at any boundary-layer angle), and the sub-mechanism taxonomy at this
single radius reduces to the three angle-protected cores (IC-direct,
OPBC-single, OL-stalled) with no PBC-correction sub-types.  The
r = 0.500 radius demonstrates, by its absence, that the 6-member
taxonomy at N >= 48 is a PBC effect: remove the PBC asymmetry and the
taxonomy simplifies to 3.

Small radii (r <= 0.050) approach a second degenerate limit.  When
the ring is very small, all particles are far from all PBC walls, and
the circumradius is much smaller than the box size.  Edge proximity
and corner proximity are both negligible, and the mechanism is
determined entirely by the angle partition.  At r = 0.025, the
boundary-band center is near gamma = 0 (no inward PBC bias) and the
sub-mechanism taxonomy in the inward sector is nearly 100% IC-direct.
This is the infinite-plane limit: as the ring shrinks relative to the
box, PBC effects diminish and the manifold approaches the ideal
two-coordinate description.

Large radii (r >= 0.900) approach a third degenerate limit.  When the
ring circumradius exceeds the box size, particles wrap around the PBC
domain multiple times, and the ring's geometry is dominated by PBC
interactions at every point.  The distinction between edge proximity
and corner proximity becomes meaningless because every particle is
simultaneously near multiple edges and corners.  At r = 0.950, the
sub-mechanism taxonomy is enriched: IC-bounced and CR-diagonal both
appear at elevated rates because PBC interactions are ubiquitous.
This is the strong-PBC limit, and it produces the widest boundary
band and the highest sub-mechanism diversity within the lambda << 1
regime.

The N = 4 folded regime remains outside Law XII's scope in the same
sense as Laws X and XI: at lambda >> 1, the trajectory complexity of
the folded manifold overrides the simple proximity analysis.  PBC
geometry at N = 4 operates through full trajectory deflection, not
through initial-configuration proximity, and the resulting mechanism
atlas has 9 types, 3 folds, 8 cusps, and 5 topological holes that
resist description as a radius-modulated angle partition.  Law XII's
three-coordinate hierarchy is the lambda <= 1 description; the
lambda >> 1 regime is governed by trajectory dynamics that require
the full Laws I through VII.

The 10 total sub-types across N = 4 to 128 are fully accounted for
by the three-coordinate hierarchy plus the lambda >> 1 trajectory
regime.  No sub-type exists that cannot be traced to either (a) the
angle partition (IC-direct, OPBC-single, OL-stalled as cores),
(b) PBC edge proximity (IC-bounced, OPBC-glancing as corrections),
(c) PBC corner proximity (CR-diagonal), (d) PBC trajectory effects
at lambda >> 1 (OPBC-winding, DE-tangent, DE-frozen), or (e) PBC
resonance at lambda ~ 1 (OL-wandering).  The taxonomy is closed.


Closing Synthesis

Law XII completes the generative description of the ED-Arch manifold
by establishing PBC geometry as the structural origin of every feature
that resists the two-coordinate reduction of Laws IX and X.

The manifold's generative hierarchy has three levels.  Lambda is the
primary coordinate.  It determines whether the manifold is folded
(lambda >> 1, N = 4), resonant (lambda ~ 1, N = 8), or planar
(lambda << 1, N >= 20).  It controls the collapse timescale, the
trajectory length, the taxonomic diversity, and the drift sensitivity.
Every property that changes monotonically with N -- curvature, torsion,
chi, entropy, drift rigidity -- is a function of lambda.

Angle is the secondary coordinate.  It determines which mechanism
sector each cell occupies: inward-collapse at positive angles,
outward-PBC at negative angles, and a stalled boundary layer near
gamma = 0.  The angle partition is topologically protected: it
persists unchanged across all N and all lambda values because it is
set by the velocity decomposition, not by the dynamics.  It is the
invariant structure that survives when lambda erases everything else.

PBC geometry, mediated by radius, is the tertiary coordinate.  It
determines the fine structure within each mechanism sector and the
transition geometry between sectors.  In the inward sector, it splits
IC-direct from IC-bounced.  In the outward sector, it sustains
CR-diagonal and OPBC-glancing as permanent minorities.  In the
boundary band, it shifts the IC/non-IC transition by up to +/-0.25
degrees and determines, cell by cell, which side of the transition
each (radius, drift) combination lands on.  PBC geometry is the
reason the negative-angle sector never becomes pure OPBC (CR-diagonal
and OPBC-glancing persist at 7-9% of the outward sector), the reason
the inward sector never becomes pure IC (5.5% non-IC from PBC
redirections), and the reason the boundary band never converges to
a line (the PBC biases that smear the boundary are independent of
lambda and N).

The three coordinates are not equal.  Lambda explains approximately
99.9% of the temporal structure (chi at floor for all lambda < 1
cells).  Angle explains approximately 94-97% of the mechanism
structure in the bulk sectors.  PBC geometry explains the remaining
3-6% in the bulk and the full 30% mechanism-prediction residual in
the boundary band.  The hierarchy is strict: removing lambda makes
the manifold unpredictable; removing angle makes the mechanism
unpredictable; removing PBC geometry makes only the fine structure
and the boundary-band position unpredictable.  Each coordinate
governs a successively finer layer of the manifold, and no layer is
redundant.

PBC geometry is the irreducible cost of simulating particles on a
periodic domain.  On an infinite plane, the manifold would be a
two-coordinate step function.  The periodic box wraps this step
function around itself, creating images that bias trajectories,
redirect collapses, and smear the phase boundary into a band.  Every
sub-mechanism variant, every mechanism minority, and every
non-convergent feature in the asymptotic regime is a consequence of
this wrapping.  PBC geometry is not a correction to the manifold.  It
is the third coordinate of the manifold.

The ED-Arch manifold is fully generated by three coordinates: lambda
tells it when to collapse, angle tells it how, and PBC geometry tells
it through which walls.
